I want to ground this post in real numbers, because the figures coming out of the AI industry right now are genuinely staggering — and understanding what they mean is important for any business leader making decisions about AI strategy.

OpenAI has surpassed **$25 billion in annualised revenue**. Anthropic is approaching **$19 billion**. Meta committed **$115–135 billion in AI capital expenditure for 2026 alone** — nearly double last year's figure. These are not projections. These are current run-rates and committed capital.

To put that in context: $135 billion is more than the entire GDP of many countries. It's being spent by a single company, in a single year, on a single technology area.

> **The platform layer of the AI economy is consolidating around a handful of companies spending at a scale that has no historical precedent. For businesses, this creates both urgency and opportunity — but the window to build AI-native competitive advantage is not unlimited.**

## Making Sense of the Numbers

Let me break these figures down into what they actually represent, because raw numbers without context can feel abstract.

### OpenAI: $25 Billion Annualised Revenue

Two years ago, OpenAI was primarily known as a research lab that made ChatGPT. Today it's a company on track for $25 billion in revenue — driven by ChatGPT subscriptions, API revenue from businesses, and enterprise contracts.

For reference: Microsoft took 20 years to reach $25 billion in annual revenue after its commercial launch. It took Google 8 years. OpenAI appears to have done it in approximately 2 years from commercial launch.

The revenue figure tells you something important: **AI is no longer a technology being evaluated by enterprises — it's being paid for at scale.** Companies are committing real budget to AI, not just running pilots.

### Anthropic: Approaching $19 Billion

Anthropic's trajectory is equally remarkable given that they launched Claude commercially significantly later than OpenAI launched GPT. The $19 billion run-rate reflects strong enterprise adoption of Claude — particularly in regulated industries where Anthropic's emphasis on safety and transparency has been a differentiator.

Amazon has committed $4 billion to Anthropic. Google has committed $2 billion. These are strategic investments from companies that have every reason to build competing models — the fact that they're also investing in Anthropic signals confidence in Anthropic's trajectory.

### Meta's $115–135 Billion AI Capex

This is the number that requires the most unpacking, because capex (capital expenditure) is different from revenue. Meta isn't earning $135 billion from AI — they're *spending* $135 billion to build the AI infrastructure they believe will underpin the next era of their business.

This spending is going into:
- Data centre construction at unprecedented scale
- GPU procurement (NVIDIA and custom silicon)
- Power infrastructure (AI data centres require enormous electricity)
- Networking and storage infrastructure
- Research and development

Why would Meta spend this much? Because their core business — social media advertising — is existentially dependent on AI for content ranking, recommendation, ad targeting, and content moderation. And because they believe the next generation of computing — AI-native applications, AR/VR, ambient computing — will require AI infrastructure as its foundational layer. They're betting the company that they'll need this infrastructure, and that having it will be a structural advantage.

## The Consolidation Pattern: What History Tells Us

The concentration of capital at the top of the AI stack follows a familiar pattern from previous technology platform shifts. Understanding the pattern helps you understand what comes next.

### The Cloud Computing Parallel

In the early 2010s, cloud computing capital concentrated rapidly around Amazon AWS, Microsoft Azure, and Google Cloud. Massive infrastructure investments by these three companies created barriers to entry that made it essentially impossible for new entrants to compete at the platform layer. The platform consolidated; the application layer thrived.

Thousands of companies built profitable businesses *on* AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud — without needing to build their own data centres. The platform investment enabled the application economy.

**AI is following the same pattern.** The infrastructure and foundation model layer is consolidating around OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta, and Microsoft. The application layer — products and services built on these models — is wide open.

### What This Means for Businesses Not Named OpenAI or Meta

For the vast majority of businesses, the consolidation at the platform layer is actually good news. Here's why:

**You don't need to build the platform.** Just as businesses in the 2010s didn't need to build their own cloud infrastructure, businesses in the 2020s don't need to train their own foundation models. The platforms being built at enormous expense by OpenAI, Anthropic, and Meta will be available to you via API — the same way AWS is available to any business.

**Competition at the platform layer drives prices down.** When multiple well-funded competitors (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta, Amazon Bedrock, Mistral) are all competing for your API business, prices fall. We're already seeing this — the cost per capable AI token has dropped by 60–100× in 24 months and continues to fall.

**The competitive advantage shifts to the application layer.** When platform capability is widely accessible, the differentiator is no longer "do we have access to AI?" — it's "are we using AI better than our competitors for our specific use case?" This is a competition where domain expertise, data, and operational discipline matter more than capital.

## The Window That's Open Right Now

Here's the urgent part of this story.

When a new platform technology consolidates, there's typically a window — usually 3–7 years — where early movers in specific application areas build durable competitive advantages that late movers struggle to overcome.

In cloud computing, the companies that built cloud-native applications and operating models in 2012–2016 gained structural advantages in cost, scale, and speed that were very hard for later entrants to match. The window wasn't unlimited.

In AI, that window is open right now. The platform layer has the foundational models. The tooling layer is maturing rapidly (APIs, MCP, agent frameworks). The application layer is still wide open.

The businesses that build AI-native workflows, AI-native customer experiences, and AI-native operational models in 2025–2027 will have compounding advantages over competitors who wait.

| Stage | Cloud Computing | AI |
|---|---|---|
| Platform foundation | 2006–2010 (AWS launch to maturity) | 2022–2024 (GPT-3 to GPT-4 era) |
| Early mover advantage window | 2010–2016 | **2024–2027 (now)** |
| Mainstream adoption | 2016–2020 | 2027–2030 (projected) |
| Commoditised baseline | 2020+ | 2030+ (projected) |

We are inside the early mover window right now.

## What "AI-Native Competitive Advantage" Actually Means

I want to be concrete about what building competitive advantage with AI actually looks like for different types of businesses — not theoretical, but operational.

### For B2B Service Companies

AI-native competitive advantage looks like: faster proposal generation (from days to hours), more consistent service delivery (AI ensures quality standards), better client data leverage (AI surfaces insights from all client interactions), and lower cost delivery (AI handles the routine, humans handle the complex).

A consultancy that can deliver the same quality of work at 40% lower cost — because AI handles research, documentation, and analysis — can either compete on price or earn better margins. Either is an advantage.

### For E-commerce and Retail

AI-native competitive advantage looks like: personalised product recommendations at scale, dynamic pricing that responds to real-time demand, AI-powered customer support that resolves 80% of queries without human intervention, and inventory forecasting that reduces stockouts and overstock simultaneously.

The companies building these capabilities now will have cost structures that competitors who haven't automated will find impossible to match.

### For Financial Services

AI-native competitive advantage looks like: faster loan underwriting (from days to minutes for standard applications), AI-powered fraud detection that catches patterns humans miss, personalised financial advice at scale (giving every customer the kind of analysis previously available only to HNI clients), and compliance workflows that flag issues before they become regulatory problems.

### For Healthcare and Wellness

AI-native competitive advantage looks like: administrative automation that reduces the burden on clinical staff, AI-assisted triage that routes patients to the right level of care, documentation generation that reduces physicians' note-taking time, and population health analytics that identify at-risk patients before they require emergency care.

## The Risk of Waiting

I want to be direct about what the investment figures at the top of this post imply about timing.

When OpenAI earns $25 billion in annualised revenue, it means their customers — businesses just like yours — are paying for AI at scale. When Meta spends $135 billion on AI infrastructure, it means they believe AI will be fundamental to commerce, communication, and computing for the next decade.

The question isn't whether AI will reshape your industry. The scale of investment makes that near-certain. The question is whether you'll be a company that shaped how AI is applied in your industry, or one that adapted to how others applied it.

The first position is structurally better. It lets you define the workflows, build the data advantages, develop the internal capabilities, and establish the customer expectations — on your terms and timeline.

The second position is reactive. It means catching up to competitors who moved first, often at higher cost and with less control.

> **The AI investment race tells you what the largest, most informed players in the world believe about the next decade. Ignore that signal at considerable strategic risk.**
